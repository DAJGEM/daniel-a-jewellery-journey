Act I — Mine the Gold

Every piece of fine jewellery begins underground. Gold hides in veins of quartz and rock, often just a few grams for every tonne of ore removed. Strike the veins to free the nuggets and watch your gold add up — this is the raw material every ring, chain and pendant starts from before a jeweller ever touches it.

Act II — Purify in the Furnace

Raw gold is never pure. To refine it, it's heated until it melts — and gold melts at exactly 1,064 °C. Hold the heat and watch the metal glow through the real colours of temperature: dull red, orange, then brilliant yellow-white as it turns liquid. What flows out is 99.99% pure gold, ready to be alloyed into something you can wear.

Act III — Choose Your Alloy

Pure 24K gold is too soft for everyday wear, so jewellers blend it with other metals to add strength and colour. More alloy means a lower karat but a harder, more durable piece. 14K and 18K are the everyday standards in Canada; white gold is plated with rhodium for its bright finish; rose gold gets its blush from copper. Choose your karat and colour and see how hardness, durability and metal value change in real time.

Act IV — Cast the Piece

Most fine jewellery is made by lost-wax casting: a wax model is encased in plaster, melted away, and the empty cavity is filled with molten metal. As it cools, the metal shrinks by roughly 1.7% — so every mould is designed slightly oversize to land on an exact fit. Choose a form and pour the gold you just refined into its mould.

Act V — Hand Finishing

A raw casting is rough and dull. Hand finishing is where it becomes jewellery — polished to a mirror, softened to satin, textured with a brushed or hammered surface, and personalised with engraving. This is craft done on-site, by hand, one piece at a time. Choose a finish and add an engraving to make it yours.

Act VI — Set the Stones

A stone is graded on the 4 Cs — carat, cut, colour and clarity — and cut matters most, because cut is what makes a diamond sparkle. Lab-grown diamonds are chemically and optically identical to natural ones for a fraction of the price; moissanite offers even more fire; sapphires, rubies and emeralds bring colour. Choose your stone, set the carat, and place it in the setting.

Act VII — Under the Microscope

Quality lives in the details you can't see with the naked eye. Under magnification a piece reveals its hallmark and purity stamp, any laser inscription, the grain structure of the alloy, and — inside a natural stone — the tiny inclusions that prove how it grew. This is the magnification we inspect every piece at. Zoom in and see for yourself.

Act VIII — Prove It's Real

Real gold behaves in ways fakes can't imitate. It won't react to acid, it isn't magnetic, its density is unmistakable, and an XRF scanner reads its exact composition in seconds. Run each professional test on a genuine piece and a plated fake, and see precisely why the fake fails.

Questions we answer every day

How do jewellers test if gold is real?

Jewellers use several tests together: an acid test (real gold doesn't react), a magnet test (gold isn't magnetic), a density or specific-gravity test by water displacement, and — most precisely — an XRF scanner that reads the exact metal composition without damaging the piece. No single test is proof on its own, but together they're conclusive.

Is a lab-grown diamond a real diamond?

Yes. A lab-grown diamond is chemically, physically and optically identical to a mined diamond — it's the same crystal, just grown in weeks instead of over a billion years. Only specialised equipment can tell them apart. Lab-grown diamonds typically cost far less, which is why many people choose them for a larger or higher-quality stone within the same budget.

What karat of gold is best for a ring?

For a ring worn every day, 14K and 18K are the sweet spot. 14K (58.5% gold) is the hardest and most affordable, ideal for active hands. 18K (75% gold) has a richer colour and is still durable enough for daily wear. 22K and 24K are softer and better suited to pieces that aren't knocked about, like earrings or investment jewellery.

Why does white gold need rhodium plating?

White gold is naturally a slightly warm, greyish colour because it's still mostly gold. A thin plating of rhodium — a bright white precious metal — gives it that crisp, platinum-like finish. The plating gradually wears with use, so most white gold pieces benefit from re-plating every one to two years to stay bright.

What temperature does gold melt at?

Pure gold melts at 1,064 °C (1,948 °F). Alloying it with other metals changes the melting point slightly, but 1,064 °C is the reference. That's why gold jewellery can be repaired, resized and recast — controlled heat lets a trained jeweller reshape it without harming the metal itself.

What is lost-wax casting?

Lost-wax casting is the centuries-old method behind most fine jewellery. A wax model of the piece is coated in plaster to make a mould, then the wax is melted and burned away, leaving a hollow cavity. Molten metal is poured or forced into that cavity, cooled, and released as a solid piece — ready for finishing, stone setting and engraving.
